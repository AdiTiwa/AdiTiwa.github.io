The Book Thief

Authored by Markus Zusak

 The story of “The Book Thief” is mainly about the protagonist of the story, Liesel Meminger, and how she grew and changed throughout her experiences in the now destroyed Nazi Germany. A theme that is prevalent through the duration of “The Book Thief” is the effect of anger and abandonment to create change, negative or positive. The story has many instances where a character experiences anger or feels abandoned through their dialogue and actions, including Liesel herself- usually leading to a change in the character’s motivations or biases.
  The first appearance of the theme is when Liesel lost her brother and her mother was taken away due to her being a communist, in which she feels both angry and feels like her family has abandoned her. She responds to this blatantly in the prologue when “She knelt. The Book Thief howled.” (page 14- prologue) in response to her brother dying. This was introduced as early as the prologue and the resentment did not stop for the protagonist of the story. However, the largest display of anger and abandonment was found late in the story, during the book burning festival. The festival took place on pages 108 to 122 in part 2, “the shoulder shrug”. After the bonfire is complete, and Liesel is talking about what she had heard during the festival, she realizes what communism is and what may have had to her biological mother. In reaction to this realization, the story recounts: “‘I hate the Fuhrer,’ she said. ‘I hate him.’”(page 155, the shoulder shrug). This moment changes Liesel’s perspective on the Nazi Party since, after this moment, she becomes more apprehensive of the actions of Nazi Germany.
  All in all, the theme that the book thief develops is that anger and abandonment can lead to change or the changing of one’s perspective. The theme is developed through the characters adapting to their environment after showing their anger or resentment towards an action or group and changing their actions or biases against that group or thought.

  “The Book Thief” was written from the perspective of a personified Death, which makes more sense to the reader later on as the book reveals its more morbid events. However, through all of these events, death is developed as a person as a character may be. But the larger undertaking was introducing the narrator in an interesting way without confusing the reader and also leaving the book to interpretation, therefore leaving the reader with the option to infer about the narrator right out the gate or ignore the identity storyteller until it is nearly explicitly told and the identity becomes necessary to a full understanding of the story. One of the first lines in the book is Death making a statement; “Here’s a small fact- You are going to die.” (page 3- prologue). Immediately, even if the reader is not sure who the narrator is going to be, it heavily insinuates someone who is close to the concept of death. However, nearly immediately after he says “Reaction to the aforementioned fact; Does this worry you? I urge you- don’t be afraid. I’m nothing but fair.” (page 3- prologue). This explanation almost seems like Death himself was consoling people that he has to eventually deal with, and the quote confirms that by referring to the past sentences and using personal pronouns with commonly used phrases to replace the idea Death with a pronoun.
  However, this can be missed by not-so-avid readers. As a result, some parts of the story may be left underdeveloped and may not make sense without the information that Death is telling a story. But, to combat these misunderstandings, the author writes about an event a handful of times, since each event is individually important to the story. Thus, even if the reader were to miss all of the hints that elude the real identity of the storyteller, the events will get clarified in the story. The result of such clarifications leads to a slightly underdeveloped story, though arguably complete story. However, most readers read the introduction as expected leading to a more interesting story where the reader has to consciously understand the many clues the author gives, making them into a more involved reader who has to put all the clues they found together to make a complete and much more meaningful, developed story.

the narrator

 Throughout the story of “The Book Thief” the author, Marcus Zusak, writes in short, chapter-like pieces to tell the story. Each chapter is nearly independent, other than the characters which are carried throughout the entire story. In addition to the shorter chapter-like stories, the book is split into 10 different parts including the prologue and epilogue. In total, there are 88 chapters in the story. However, there is a problem with this sort of structure- events can be broken up without context to connect events to make such transitions confusing for the reader. Some parts of the story include large gaps of time between the last event and the current chapter’s events. These can lead to a discontinued understanding of the story and the story’s parts. One example of this is the beginning of the first part (“the grave digger’s handbook”) in which right at the beginning of the first chapter where Death recounts the story that was told in the prologue, specifically when Liesel lost her brother on trying to travel to Himmel Street. Even if the period of time that the chapter covers is not far from the time that Liesel’s brother died, it was a break in the story that could have confused some readers.
  To combat such discontinuation, the author uses techniques to help the readers that may have skipped important parts of the story, and in this case the epilogue, since the epilogue is usually considered a non-necessary part of the story. On page 20 (the grave digger’s handbook) Death referred to the events that were described in the epilogue, effectively repeating the story that was told before but faster and in a way that doesn’t require a lot of inferring due to the sheer number of hints provided. This is a strategy that the author used a lot throughout the story, recounting past events to remind the reader or to bring the reader up to speed on certain plot affecting events. Such repetitive storytelling is effective here since the reader gets a shorter, more distilled version of the events when Liesel’s brother died without having to read the epilogue which describes the event in more detail but has more elements that are vague and require more inference to understand. In addition to the clues that nearly explicitly tell you the events that transpired in the epilogue, the reader gets new information about the plot of the current chapter since the plot can change really quickly if the reader doesn’t understand the last chapter’s content. In finishing, this strategy doesn’t take up much time for the reader however it can aid many readers that missed important details while including new information for the new events in the current chapter.

 Throughout “The Book Thief”, many people were discriminated against, due to their religion or heritage or the government structure that they followed. The main character of the story, Liesel, was shown to have communist parents. Therefore, they were taken away and Liesel and her unnamed brother were orphaned. Such discrimination is also in effect at this time, now mostly about someone’s socioeconomic position and their race and not as much about the religion that they follow.

 Liesel, for a good portion of the book, is spending time at school, and the way the school was developed seemed similar to older school systems. Also, as it seems in the story, the schools had different classes near the same way that many people currently have an elementary school. However, Liesel is 12 in a good portion of the book, leading to questions about eh school structure in Nazi Germany. This was probably told in the book to show how the world that Liesel lived in was not as different from our world and time.

 From Death’s monologue in the epilogue, he began to state that the only thing that is keeping him going is the colors he sees on occasion. However, in real life, it seems as though the colors of the sky do not change (ignoring sunsets). In any case, real-life actually have differing sky colors depending on the location you are viewing from. Thus, we can assume that Death has his work all around the world, which further proves his identity.

  I, having read “The Book Thief” would rate the book 4.5/5 stars. It has a great story and has a creative cast of characters. I remarkably enjoyed the way that each chapter either developed the entire story or shot off in another direction, leading to very interesting storylines to follow. However, sometimes I am not much of a fan of the fluff off the main story. Many events have near to no effect on the characters and the story seems to be at a standstill, though there are very minimal chapters like that. Overall I would recommend this book to everyone, as long as they can handle harsh language and sometimes morbid scenes. It is a great book specifically for those who enjoy historical fiction since it takes actual events from the time and mixes it into the story.
